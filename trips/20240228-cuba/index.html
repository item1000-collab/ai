This toll booth (PEAJE) marks the entrance to the Varadero peninsula, one of Cuba's most famous beach destinations. Stretching 20 km along the Hicacos Peninsula, Varadero is renowned for its fine white sand and crystal-clear turquoise waters. 007.webp — Cuban Countryside Hospitality & White Rubber Boots
Taken from a moving car, a man in a light shirt waves warmly at the camera while a woman stands near the porch entrance. Notice the man's white rubber boots (botas de goma) — a distinctive and practical footwear choice deeply rooted in Cuban rural life. These boots are essential for farmers, tobacco workers, and anyone working in muddy fields, wetlands, or during the rainy season. The white color helps keep feet cooler under the intense Caribbean sun. Often associated with vaqueros (cowboys) and agricultural workers, rubber boots symbolize the hardworking spirit of Cuba's countryside. They protect against mud, water, and even snakes, making them ubiquitous from the tobacco fields of Pinar del Río to the sugarcane plantations of the east. The simple fence, tropical shrubs, and genuine smile capture authentic Cuban hospitality in rural life.

011.webp — Plaza Mayor, Trinidad (UNESCO World Heritage)
This cobblestone square with a large tree bursting with bright pink bougainvillea is the heart of Trinidad, one of Cuba's best-preserved colonial cities. Founded in 1514, Trinidad is a UNESCO World Heritage site, famous for its cobblestone streets, pastel-colored mansions, and timeless atmosphere. The light blue colonial building with white trim is typical of the city's architecture. The vibrant bougainvillea — a signature of Trinidad — frames the scene as groups of tourists take in the historic charm. The town was once a wealthy sugar-trade hub, and its central plaza remains frozen in the 19th century.

012.webp — Paseo del Prado, Cienfuegos (The Pearl of the South)
This elegant pedestrian boulevard is the famous Paseo del Prado in Cienfuegos, a city known as "La Perla del Sur" (The Pearl of the South). Founded by French immigrants in 1819, Cienfuegos boasts a unique architectural heritage blending neoclassical, Art Deco, and colonial styles. The wide central promenade is lined with slender palm trees, wrought-iron benches, and meticulously restored buildings with arched galleries. Unlike Havana's more congested streets, Cienfuegos offers a spacious, airy elegance. The boulevard leads toward the city's historic core, a UNESCO World Heritage site, and reflects the city's nickname — refined, luminous, and distinctly Caribbean with a French touch.

016.webp — Bacunayagua Bridge — Cuba's Highest Bridge
This dramatic concrete bridge curving across a deep green valley is the Puente de Bacunayagua (Bacunayagua Bridge), located 18 km west of Matanzas on the Vía Blanca highway. Completed in 1959 and inaugurated by Fidel Castro on September 26 of that year, it is the highest bridge in Cuba at 103.5 meters (340 feet) above the river below. With a total length of 313.5 meters and an arch span of 114 meters, it is considered one of the seven wonders of Cuban civil engineering. The bridge connects Mayabeque and Matanzas provinces and carries the Vía Blanca across the lush Yumurí Valley canyon. It was designed by Cuban engineer Luis Sáenz Duplace and built by the firm SACMAG using the innovative Melan system (semi-arches), the first time this Austrian technique was used in Cuba. The dense jungle canopy below and gray sky overhead emphasize the awe-inspiring scale of this engineering marvel.

016 · Bacunayagua Bridge · Cuba's Tallest Bridge · Engineering WonderView original →

016-1.jpg — Underside View of Bacunayagua Bridge
This dramatic underside view showcases the massive concrete viaduct of the Bacunayagua Bridge, with its thick pillars and elegant steel-reinforced arches stretching over a forested valley. The bridge was built between 1956 and 1959 using prefabricated techniques and post-tensioned concrete. The towers are built on the Vierendel truss principle, and the 47-ton beams were placed using launching trusses because Cuba at the time had no cranes capable of lifting such weight. The cloudy sky adds moody grandeur to this engineering masterpiece, which offers breathtaking views of the Yumurí Valley to the south and glimpses of the Caribbean Sea to the north. A popular tourist stop, there is a mirador (lookout) with a snack bar on the western side of the bridge.

018.webp — Plaza Vieja, Havana
This expansive civic square surrounded by colonnaded buildings is Plaza Vieja (Old Square) in Havana, one of the city's most architecturally diverse plazas. Built in 1559, it was originally a parade ground and market. Today, it blends Baroque, Art Nouveau, and Neo-Moorish styles. The large leafy tree on the left provides shade for groups of pedestrians, while the surrounding buildings house art galleries, cafés, and apartments. After decades of neglect, Plaza Vieja was fully restored in the 1990s and now stands as one of Havana's most beloved gathering spots, a perfect example of the city's living history.

020.webp — El Capitolio Nacional, Havana
The iconic El Capitolio in Havana is one of the city's most recognizable landmarks. Completed in 1929, it was inspired by the Panthéon in Paris and resembles the U.S. Capitol, though it stands taller. Its golden dome, white limestone facade, classical columns, towering palms, and vintage lampposts create a stunning composition. The building housed Cuba's congress until the 1959 revolution and now serves as the headquarters of the Cuban Academy of Sciences and a major tourist attraction. Under bright sunlight with fluffy clouds overhead, El Capitolio remains the undisputed symbol of Havana's grandeur and republican era.
