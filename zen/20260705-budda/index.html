The Mystery of the ‘Fire’ Buddha

What gestures and halos reveal — a journey through Buddhist iconography with Gemini AI
🔍 investigation ✧ AI-assisted April 2026

After returning from a DPRK tour, we reviewed our footage. What began as a simple photo review with Google’s Gemini turned into a full-scale art-historical investigation — all thanks to a golden statue and a flame above a Buddha’s head.

Geography by halo

The shape of the “flame” (the radiant nimbus) can pinpoint a statue's origin with surprising accuracy.

🇹🇭 Thai Buddhas elongated, tongue‑like flame, sharp and tall.
🇨🇳🇰🇷 East Asian rounder, more stylized halo · soft contour.

Our team quickly ruled out Thai origin. The drapery and the halo's curve pointed unmistakably to East Asia — Korea or China.

“Usually Buddha comes alone, without other figures.” — a common misconception.
In East Asian temples, the Buddha is almost always accompanied by two bodhisattvas: Wisdom and Compassion. This classic triad format is not unusual — it signals deep tradition.

Language without words: the hands of Buddha

The most gripping part of the discussion revolved around mudrās — symbolic hand gestures. Every finger position carries sacred meaning. Thanks to screen‑sharing, we could examine each hand in detail.

🖐️ Abhaya‑mudrā · right hand raised, palm outward — fearlessness, protection
✋ Dāna‑mudrā · left hand on knee, palm up — generosity, openness to teaching
“I can't even do that with my own hand!” — Peter, trying to replicate the left attendant's gesture. — Gemini identified it as a variation of the Abhaya‑mudrā (fearlessness).

The figure on the left displayed an unusual raised hand — a position that seemed almost impossible to mimic. Yet Gemini recognised it as another form of the fearlessness gesture, confirming that even unconventional poses belong to a coherent iconographic system.

What the triad tells us

Left and right figures — Wisdom and Compassion — frame the central Buddha. This triad structure is a hallmark of Mahayana Buddhism and is especially common in Korean and Chinese temples. The central Buddha often represents Vairocana or Shakyamuni, while the bodhisattvas embody the active principles of enlightenment.

🧠 AI meets intuition

Gemini provided real‑time iconographic clues, but it was the human eye — Peter's zoom, the group's curiosity — that turned a casual photo review into a masterclass in Buddhist iconography.
