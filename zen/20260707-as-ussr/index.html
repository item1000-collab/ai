As if in the USSR again:
why Pyongyang reminds of Moscow in the 80s

A time capsule of socialist architecture, electric transport, and the cult of leaders
Do you know that feeling — when you step into a place that feels both incredibly distant and painfully familiar? That’s exactly what tourists experience when they set foot in Pyongyang. At first glance, the DPRK capital revives images of Soviet Moscow.

Pyongyang’s architecture — majestic Stalinist high‑rises, wide avenues, and an abundance of monuments. It strongly resembles Moscow of the 70s–80s, only in a more modern and “groomed” version. Yet the similarity is not only external.

🚗 private cars
Private cars were only allowed to be purchased one year ago. Today, most are Chinese models.
⚡ public transport
Almost fully electric: electric buses, trolleybuses, and trams — a nostalgic glimpse of a cleaner urban past.

Another nostalgic memory — the attitude toward the leaders.

If in the USSR we saw portraits of Lenin, here — Kim Il‑sung and Kim Jong‑il. And while in the Soviet Union badges with Lenin were worn mostly by Octobrists and Pioneers, here badges with the leaders are worn by almost everyone. It’s not a party card, but a mark of distinction awarded for merit.

Pyongyang is like a time machine. You can see things that in Russia remain only in history textbooks and in the memory of the older generation.

But at the same time, North Korean socialism has followed its own unique path. The capital breathes with a different rhythm — disciplined, monumental, and eerily quiet, yet at every step it echoes the Soviet era that once was.

🏛️ From the iconic Ryugyong Hotel to the Grand Monument on Mansu Hill — every structure feels like a page from a Soviet architectural album, but with a distinct Korean imprint.

✧ The past and the present merge in Pyongyang’s wide avenues — a living museum of a bygone ideology, yet strangely modern in its own way.
