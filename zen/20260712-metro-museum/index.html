Pyongyang Metro Construction Museum: A Glimpse into North Korea’s Underground Infrastructure

In June 2026, a visit was made to the Metro Construction Museum in Pyongyang, the capital of the Democratic People's Republic of Korea. Located in a monumental building in the city center, the museum is not merely an exhibition space but a comprehensive presentation of the phases involved in building one of the deepest and most unique subway systems in the world.

Exterior Architecture

The museum building features a strict, monumental architectural style typical of Pyongyang’s public structures. The facade is clad in light-colored stone, with reddish-brown columns and a prominent golden inscription in Korean on the pediment, denoting the facility's purpose. Broad stone steps lead up to the main entrance, where groups of visitors typically assemble before entering.

Main facade and entrance of the museum.

Exhibits on Manual Labor and Construction

The interior halls are divided into thematic zones. One of the core sections focuses on the early stages of construction, which were carried out with extensive manual labor under difficult geological conditions. Dioramas featuring realistic mannequins in hard hats and work uniforms recreate scenes of concrete mixing, pouring, and tunnel wall reinforcement. While there are no interactive digital elements, these life-sized sculptures and wide panoramic displays offer a vivid depiction of mid-20th-century tunneling techniques.

Mannequins demonstrating manual concrete pouring in a simulated tunnel environment.
A massive panoramic painting depicting hundreds of workers in a flooded tunnel section.

Panoramic Depictions of Scale

Several exhibition halls feature monumental canvases that create an immersive effect. These large-scale paintings illustrate coordinated efforts, with dozens of figures engaged in rock removal, material transport, and the installation of timber supports. Photographs and placards accompanying these artworks provide details on the technical aspects and the chronological timeline of specific station developments.

A construction diorama featuring red banners and a large workforce.
Detailed scene showing the use of wooden frameworks for tunnel support.

Interior Architecture and Lighting

Beyond the construction narratives, the museum also showcases decorative elements found on the actual metro stations. In one prominent hall, the ceiling features original lighting fixtures designed in clusters, resembling fruit bunches or spheres. The white plaster arches, intricate moldings, and multi-layered illumination system create a palatial aesthetic that stands in sharp contrast to the harsh underground scenes depicted elsewhere.

Arched ceiling with decorative molding and clustered pendant lamps.

Rolling Stock and Interior Decor

A separate pavilion houses samples of rolling stock and infrastructure components. For instance, an older bus adapted for rail movement is displayed on a dedicated stand, surrounded by stanchions to maintain visitor distance. The walls of this hall are tiled in a light green palette and feature murals of agricultural crops, reflecting common aesthetic themes found in North Korean public spaces. The flooring is polished stone, and actual railway tracks are laid out along the path.

A retro bus positioned on a rail display, surrounded by green-tiled walls with floral imagery.

City Model and Subterranean Layout

To illustrate the relationship between underground structures and the city surface, one hall features a large-scale illuminated model of Pyongyang. Nighttime lighting effects simulate the cityscape, while specific cross-sections reveal the alignment of tunnels and stations beneath major buildings and hills. This visual aid helps to convey the logistical scope and spatial depth of the underground network.

A cross-section scale model showing the metro layout beneath the city landscape.

Exit and Urban Surroundings

After completing the tour, visitors step out onto a spacious paved plaza. From this vantage point, a view of central Pyongyang is visible, including high-rise residential complexes and a green zone with mature trees. A parking area for tour buses is situated adjacent to the exit.

The plaza and urban skyline as seen from the museum's exit.

The museum is a regular stop on the itineraries of most international delegations. It offers a general overview of the scale of infrastructure projects realized in Pyongyang during the second half of the 20th century and provides an opportunity to learn about the historical evolution of urban transportation in the region.
