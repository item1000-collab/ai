“We build it ourselves, we buy it ourselves, we eat it ourselves”

How Juche theory works in real life · a travelogue AI Travel & Language Lab

When I returned from North Korea, many people asked me about the everyday life of the locals. And it is organised according to a principle that the locals call “self-reliance”. It sounds pompous. In reality — it is very wise.

🇰🇵 Their own buses and trams

Our guide, while we were standing in the square where the Juche Tower rises, explained it like this: “We try to do everything ourselves. Not to buy from others.” And you can really see it. For example, the buses are their own. They built the industry themselves, replaced imported parts with domestic ones. They are very proud of this. Before, they bought trams from Czechoslovakia; now they make their own.

🍵 Barley tea and kimchi

Speaking of food. “Self-reliance” also works in the kitchen. Breakfast, lunch and dinner — besides many other dishes — were accompanied by kimchi, fermented cabbage. There was a lot of it. It was always served.

And instead of the usual tea — a drink made from barley. The taste is specific, bready. The guide explained: “We don’t drink imported tea. We have our own.” We drank it every day. I wouldn’t say it’s a masterpiece, but it’s quite tasty.

✦ My verdict

The Juche theory — it is not only about politics. It is also about everyday life. About how they try to live their own life, brew their own tea, make their own buses …

On the background of the main symbol of the state — the Juche Monument — an ordinary citizen.

“Сами построим, сами купим, сами съедим”  — the spirit of self-reliance echoes in every detail.
