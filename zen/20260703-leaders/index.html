“Their attitude toward leaders is the most unusual” – what we saw in North Korea

✈️ AI Travel & Language Lab · from our recent tour
What life looks like in the world’s most closed country, through the eyes of eyewitnesses: from the cleanliness of Pyongyang to the deep respect for traditions.

When people talk about North Korea, stereotypes come to mind. But how do they match reality? We recently returned from a tour of the DPRK and want to share our impressions. Our observations help to see this country from a different angle.

Pyongyang: a city that surprises

The first thing that strikes you upon arrival is the cleanliness and well‑groomed appearance of the capital. Everywhere is very clean, beautiful, with splendid buildings.

Pyongyang greets guests with wide avenues, modern high‑rises and neat parks. The buildings look modern and aesthetic. There are cars on the roads — not as many as in megacities, but it’s a quite ordinary city scene. The architecture leaves a pleasant impression, and many buildings, we were told, were constructed after the Korean War.

Social traditions & family care

One of the most interesting aspects concerns social foundations. We were told that newlyweds in the DPRK can receive new housing from the state. The country takes great care of the institution of family: the average age of marriage is 24–27.

💡 Rare divorces

Divorces are a rare phenomenon here. For Korean society it is a very unusual action. People ask: why couldn’t they get along with each other? This speaks of a deep respect for family values and traditions that are passed down from generation to generation.

Rural areas: harmony with nature

Outside the capital, picturesque landscapes open up. The fields look very neat, everything is in good order. These places remind one of the Swiss Alps — green hills, villages.

In the countryside you can see a traditional way of life. For us it was an opportunity to touch a culture where people live in harmony with nature and take care of their land.

Deep respect for national leaders

“The most unusual thing for us was the deep respect with which locals treat their leaders.”

When speaking about their leaders, they always add the word “comrade”, not just the name. Portraits of the founding fathers can be seen in many public places. Locals wear badges with their images on their clothes.

We accepted these rules because we respect their feelings. It seemed unusual to us, but it looked sincere. This is part of their patriotism and attitude toward their country. For Koreans, reverence for leaders is not just a formality, but an important part of upbringing from childhood.

A journey with deep meaning

Visiting North Korea is not just a tourist trip, but an opportunity to see a unique culture that is very different from the Western one. The tour programme includes not only excursions around the capital, but also visits to Buddhist monasteries, and getting to know traditional crafts.

What we bought in the DPRK

🧵 Handmade treasures

North Korea is famous for its handmade products. We bought two embroidered pictures. They are very beautiful. One — with symbolic flowers, the other — with a girl in traditional costume. We worried about their safety during the flight, but they now adorn our homes safely.

Conclusion: a country with rich culture and traditions

Our trip showed that the reality of North Korea is multifaceted. Pyongyang surprises with modern architecture and cleanliness, and beyond its borders a serene rural nature unfolds. The people of the country respect their history, traditions and family.

It was a very interesting journey. We saw a country that lives by its own rules, and that deserves respect. Our experience is yet another proof that any culture is better studied not through stereotypes, but through personal impressions.
