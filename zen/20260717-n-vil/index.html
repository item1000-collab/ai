National Village by the Sea
Night in a Korean Minbak

Traditional guesthouses, warm ondol floors, and the rhythm of coastal life
📅 17 July 2026 📍 Chilbosan · East Coast ⏱ 6 min read

We spent a night in a national village directly on the coast — not a standard hotel, but a place where visitors step into the everyday life of rural Korea. We stayed in traditional Korean houses (locally called minbak — private guesthouses) side by side with local families.

The hostesses — small, slender Korean women — welcomed us with warm smiles and did everything to make us feel at home.

What is a Minbak and how it works

These national villages — especially in the Chilbosan mountain region on North Korea’s east coast — were developed by the state to boost domestic tourism and showcase traditional ways of life. Unlike large hotels, there are no anonymous rooms. Visitors stay in houses where one part is occupied by a local family, and the other is rented to guests. This lets travellers see real daily life while providing extra income to residents.

🛏 Ondol · heated floors — Some houses had conventional beds, others had traditional futons spread directly on the floor. But every room shared one feature: the floors were astonishingly warm. That's because of the ancient ondol underfloor heating system, used by Koreans for around five hundred years.

“The floor radiates a gentle warmth, as if the house itself is embracing you.”

Workshops & traditional life

The village is not just a place to sleep — it's a cultural hub. Around the houses, small vegetable gardens are cultivated, perfectly matching the agrarian aesthetic of such tourism sites.

🍜 Kuksu noodlesThick, hearty starch noodles
🍡 Sweet rice cakesSimilar to tteok, made with glutinous rice
🎲 Yut gameTraditional board game, played during holidays

Guests are invited to join cooking workshops. We learned to make kuksu — thick, hearty starch noodles. We also tried preparing a sweet glutinous rice dish, typical of this region (similar to rice cakes tteok).

The next morning, the hostess brought us a fragrant ginseng (or barley) tea and served fresh Korean pancakes with greens — a typical local breakfast.

Interior details & local colour

The interior of the houses combines traditional elements — carved wooden doors, octagonal windows — with modern conveniences: LG televisions on the walls, landline phones, and in some rooms, portraits of national leaders. This adds a distinctive, authentic atmosphere to the region.

Nature and the sea

The location is stunningly picturesque. On one side, high green mountains covered with pine forests; on the other, the sea and a sandy beach. Stone-paved paths wind through the village, with trees whose trunks are whitewashed — a common sight in well-kept Korean resorts. Despite the overcast weather, the air felt fresh, and pink roses blooming along the paths added vivid touches of colour.

This format of staying in a national village is the perfect way to see the unpolished, everyday life and immerse yourself in the culture — through food, warm floors, and traditional hospitality.
