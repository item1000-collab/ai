Kimchi, North Korean style – the taste of everyday life in the DPRK on one plate

🍲 AI Travel & Language Lab · Pyongyang, 2026
📸 A plate of traditional North Korean kimchi served in a Pyongyang restaurant · June 2026
In this photo, taken in Pyongyang, we see perhaps the most recognisable Korean dish — kimchi. Looking at this plate is a chance to touch the everyday food culture of North Korea.

What we see on the plate

Before us is a portion of cabbage, generously coated in red sauce. The red colour in Korean cuisine is not just aesthetic — it’s a signature. The sauce is based on kochukaru (red chilli powder), which gives the dish not only its colour but also its characteristic heat. Veins of garlic and ginger are also visible — essential ingredients responsible for the pungency and aroma.

🥣 The liquid gold

Notice the abundant liquid (juice) at the bottom of the plate. In North Korea this brine is called kimchi-guk (kimchi soup). It is considered just as valuable as the cabbage itself. In cold weather, this brine is often added to stews or simply eaten with rice to warm up and replenish vitamins.

Kimchi in North Korea: traditions and features

North Korean cuisine has its own specific character:

🥬 1. Seasonality & mass preparation

Kimjang — the seasonal mass salting of kimchi for winter — is a major event, comparable to a national holiday. Families, teams and even enterprises prepare hundreds of kilograms of cabbage for winter. The taste of aged winter kimchi is more sour and rich.

🧂 2. Simple recipe

For kimchi in the DPRK they use only salt, pepper, garlic, ginger and a little spring onion. Yet this simplicity allows the flavour of the cabbage to fully unfold.

🇰🇵 3. National pride

Kimchi in the DPRK is not just a side dish. It is a symbol of resilience, health and self‑sufficiency. The government emphasises its benefits, calling it an “elixir of longevity” and one of the main national treasures.

Part of the daily meal

This photo was taken in a hotel restaurant in Pyongyang. In most establishments in the DPRK, kimchi is served free of charge and in unlimited quantities as a complement to the main hot dish (for example, soup or rice).

Looking at this moist, ruddy piece of cabbage, one can say: here lives the same Korean soul that manifests itself in the love for spicy, warm and hearty food.

🍚 everyday life   In North Korea, kimchi is more than a side dish — it is a daily ritual, a connection to the land, and a taste of home. It embodies the warmth, resilience and communal spirit of Korean culture.
