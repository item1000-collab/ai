What a "People's" Supermarket Looks Like in Pyongyang

These four photographs capture an ordinary, strictly local store — seen through a lens from the outside.

This is not a place for tourists or shoppers with dollars or euros. It's a world built on domestic currency — Korean won — and strict food distribution norms. Let's take a look at the shelves.

◆ Drinks and Snacks

The first thing that catches the eye on this display is the impressive selection of alcohol. Glass bottles line the shelves in neat rows — from clear infusions to dark-brown bottles, most likely containing traditional rice wine or local insam-ju (ginseng liquor), which is considered something of a national treasure. The green and bright-yellow boxes, carefully stacked, suggest that these goods are sold not only by the bottle but also as gift sets. In the foreground, we see packages tied with twine and plastic baskets filled with small jars — perhaps delicacies like pickled seafood or wild berry preserves.

◆ Takeaway Food and the Daily Meal Routine

The second photo transports us to the atmosphere of a canteen or prepared-food section. Slanted glass display counters are the center of attention for local residents. Women in comfortable everyday clothes — some with bags, some in caps — wait in line, peering at what's on offer today. Containers are arranged neatly along the serving line, offering a real, down-to-earth selection. This is a proper meal for those stopping by after work or before their shift. Above the doorway hangs a sign reading "VICTORY" ("Processed Products / Prepared Food") — confirming that this is where locals come to solve the problem of a quick dinner.

◆ Dry Goods and Fresh Meat Aisles

The third photo reveals the busiest part of the store. Shelves stretching into the depths of the hall are packed with endless rows of cans, boxes, and bottles. This is classic grocery territory — from sauces to preserves.

Fruit liqueurs are also visible, arranged in tight rows on the shelves.

Pieces of meat (or poultry) lie right in trays — some wrapped in foil for preservation, others left open. This suggests that meat here is not a scarcity, as is often portrayed in the West, but rather an accessible — though rationally used — product.

◆ The "Green Row": Fruit Without Frills

The last photo stands out for its simplicity. A vibrant green produce counter, decorated with little cloud motifs and red script on the sign (apparently reading "Fruit Section" or "Southern Fruits"), presents fresh fruit without excessive packaging. Orange tangerines, yellow melons, and round pears sit in plastic trays right in front of customers. There are no glossy price tags in euros, no branded plastic bags — just clean, crisp seasonal produce.

Overall Impression
The main takeaway from these images is that the assortment feels as though it has been "frozen in time." There's no abundance of imported goods typical of supermarkets in any other city. Instead, there's strict functionality, the logic of food security, and a distinctly "people's" design aesthetic.

These photographs — taken through the prism of shop-window glass — seem to retain a trace of a unique atmosphere. The faint reflections and glare, inevitable when shooting through a barrier, add a sense of detachment to the scene, emphasizing that this is not a staged shot, but a genuine slice of life. This is no tourist postcard — it's an imprint of real, everyday existence: simple, functional, and surprisingly varied.
