📰 Geopolitics, resolve & escalation — based on The Economist & The National (2026-07-16 / 2026-07-18)

This lesson examines two urgent global stories: Europe’s defence psychology
and the Iran–US confrontation in the Gulf. We focus on advanced vocabulary, idioms, and thematic blocks that capture the tone of crisis and deterrence.

❶ Europe & the will to fight

⚔️ Deterrence paradox

European leaders raise defence budgets not to fight in Ukraine, but because a kinetic war beyond Ukraine seems plausible. The article argues that “spending is one thing, fighting is another” – and that societal resilience is hard to cultivate in peacetime.

🗺️ Geography, politics, media shape willingness.

🛡️ Ukrainian resolve vs European fear

A Ukrainian official: “We’re not superhuman, we’re just at war.” War transforms cognition, unleashes creativity. Europeans are abhorring war, not necessarily cowardly. But without war, preparedness fades – and that weakens deterrence.

“Can the spirit needed to deter war be summoned without fully entering one?”

❷ Iran suspends commitments · seventh night of strikes

🔥 Escalation in the Gulf

Iran suspended commitments under the Islamabad Memorandum after US strikes. Tehran hit infrastructure in Kuwait, Bahrain, Jordan – including desalination plants, airports, and oil installations. The US enforced a naval blockade, while Iran targeted shipping in the Strait of Hormuz.

💥 regional chokepoints under pressure

Kuwait, Bahrain, and Jordan – all hosting US bases – came under attack. The IRGC struck fuel piers, communications centres, and ammunition depots. US Central Command: “50,000 service members remain vigilant, lethal and ready.”

⚠️ Oil, water, and power grids targeted – civilian impact severe.

📚 Advanced vocabulary & idioms

  • kinetic war · active, armed conflict (as opposed to hybrid or cold war)
  • boots on the ground · military ground troops deployed in a theatre
  • reassurance force · troops deployed to signal solidarity, not direct combat
  • naval blockade · military effort to stop vessels entering/leaving a port
  • chokepoint · narrow strategic passage (Strait of Hormuz, Red Sea)
  • societal resilience · society’s capacity to endure & resist crisis
  • hybrid conflict · mix of conventional, cyber, and proxy warfare
🔹 from the texts: “Europeans are now the main military backers of Kyiv” · “spending is not increasing because European armies are planning to fight” · “a truce is not in sight” · “all-out war” · “testing the limits of escalation”.

📌 Summary · part 1
European nations are rearming not to directly intervene in Ukraine, but because a wider kinetic war with Russia seems increasingly plausible. However, surveys show that willingness to fight varies dramatically by geography – northern and eastern Europeans are far more prepared than southerners, partly due to media, politics, and proximity. The author, Nathalie Tocci, suggests that a European “reassurance force” deployed before a ceasefire could force citizens to confront reality, fostering the psychological resilience needed for deterrence – even if it puts troops at risk.

📌 Summary · part 2
Simultaneously, the Iran–US conflict has escalated after a collapsed ceasefire. Iran has suspended its commitments under the Islamabad Memorandum, striking infrastructure in Kuwait, Bahrain, and Jordan – targeting US bases, desalination plants, and oil facilities. The US enforces a naval blockade in the Strait of Hormuz while Iran threatens to stop all oil and gas exports from the region. Both sides exchange strikes on military and civilian sites, raising fears of a broader regional war, with Kuwait and other Gulf states caught in the crossfire.
