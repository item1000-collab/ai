🕊️ A sovereign future perspective

Why a broken Russia is bad for the world · Andrey Melnichenko
📅 July 9, 2026 · By invitation 🌍 resilience · architecture · dialogue
✨
From pressure to possibility — this text invites us to reflect on shared security, sovereignty as a bridge, and the long arc of rebuilding trust. Even through complexity, the author emphasises that sovereignty, responsibility, and dialogue remain the only solid ground for lasting peace.

1. The architecture of trust

Great wars do not begin where the first shots are fired. The front line is merely the point where accumulated pressure finally breaks through. By that moment the foundation has already been destroyed: the language of mutual security, trust in commitments, a shared understanding of what is permissible, the capacity to perceive the other side as part of a common system rather than a threat to be eliminated.
When these bonds break, politics does not direct events; it is led by them.

2. Sovereignty as condition

The central paradox of the current moment is this: the demand for international security has never been higher, yet the institutional infrastructure built to supply it—norms, enforcement bodies, shared legitimacy frameworks—has never been weaker.
Destroying sovereignty does not resolve the security problem; it removes the only mechanism through which the problem can be addressed.

Ukraine is not just a battlefield. It is a state, a society and a political will that has paid a terrible price. Ukrainian sovereignty is real. But Ukrainian security built on the permanent negation of Russia’s sovereign agency is equally unstable.
A neighbour with known interests and a predictable price for its commitments represents a different quality of security than a neighbour defined by revanchism or siege.

“Sovereignty is a necessary condition of any stable global-security architecture.”

3. Scenarios & structural logic

Versailles was not the creation of order but the accumulation of deferred energy. Russia is not Weimar Germany, but the structural logic retains its force: if the sovereignty of a major historical nation is broken, it rarely disappears. It returns in a more dangerous form.
In the second scenario, Russia ends up in China’s orbit. In the long term it simply changes the address of the dependency.

The third scenario — fragmentation — destroys the cohesion that makes nuclear deterrence functional. The final possibility: Russia as a fortress, where technology, science, capital and civic trust do not grow in a state of perpetual emergency.

4. Attrition is not a strategy

Negotiations work when both sides believe the other is able and willing to defend its position to the limit. When one side concludes that the other is bluffing, it stops seeking a solution at the table.
A war of attrition, by itself, cannot resolve the problem. It perpetuates the problem rather than resolving it.

5. The nuclear constraint

Deterrence works not because the weapons exist, but because rational decision-making centres exist, communication channels are open and both sides understand where the limits lie.
Any strategy that treats nuclear escalation as a manageable extension of conventional pressure rests on a false assumption: that a complex system can be pushed to the edge and stopped precisely where it is politically convenient. Real systems do not work that way.

6. Internal reckoning & sovereignty

How Russia conducts its own political process and towards what ends it directs its sovereignty is a question that can be resolved only inside Russia itself, without deference to external preferences.
Severe constraints — military pressure, economic sanctions, information warfare — compel efficiency. And efficiency is only possible when all social strata work together.

7. Predictability over sympathy

A sovereign Russia will not make every country comfortable. But it will be more advantageous in the long run than the alternatives. The choice is not between a friendly Russia and a hostile one. It is between a Russia whose behaviour is predictable and one whose trajectory is unknown.

The choice is between two kinds of future: one in which major powers again learn to respect each other’s sovereignty, and one in which each attempts to reduce the others to objects of management. The second path has already brought us here.

8. A closing thought

The most important thing is that we step back from the abyss. Only then can we ask how we reached it and how to arrange the world differently. That work belongs to the next generation. Our role is to ensure they have something to work with.

Deterrencepreventing an action through the threat of severe consequences.
Architecturehere: the underlying structure of a security or political system.
Structural logicthe underlying pattern that drives outcomes regardless of context.
